\n1840 (MDCCCXL) was a leap year starting on Wednesday (dominical letter ED) of the Gregorian calendar and a leap year starting on Monday (dominical letter GF) of the Julian calendar, the 1840th year of the Common Era (CE) and Anno Domini (AD) designations, the 840th year of the 2nd millennium, the 40th year of the 19th century, and the 1st year of the 1840s decade. Note that the Julian day for 1840 is 12 calendar days difference, which continued to be used from 1582 until the complete conversion of the Gregorian calendar was entirely done in 1929.

\n

Events[edit]

\n

January\xe2\x80\x93March[edit]

\n
    \n
  • January 3 \xe2\x80\x93 One of the predecessor papers of the Herald Sun of Melbourne, Australia, The Port Phillip Herald, is founded.
    • \n
  • January 10 \xe2\x80\x93 Uniform Penny Post is introduced in the United Kingdom.
    • \n
  • January 13 \xe2\x80\x93 The steamship Lexington burns and sinks in icy waters, four miles off the coast of Long Island; 139 die, only four survive.
    • \n
  • January 19 \xe2\x80\x93 Captain Charles Wilkes\' United States Exploring Expedition sights what becomes known as Wilkes Land in the southeast quadrant of Antarctica, claiming it for the United States and providing evidence that Antarctica is a complete continent.[1]
    • \n
  • January 21 \xe2\x80\x93 Jules Dumont d\'Urville discovers Ad\xc3\xa9lie Land in Antarctica, claiming it for France.[2]
    • \n
  • January 22 \xe2\x80\x93 British colonists reach New Zealand, officially founding Wellington.
    • \n
  • February \xe2\x80\x93 The Rhodes blood libel is made against the Jews of Rhodes.
    • \n
  • February 5 \xe2\x80\x93 The murder of a Capuchin friar and his Greek servant leads to the Damascus affair, a highly publicized case of blood libel against the Jews of Damascus.
    • \n
  • February 6 \xe2\x80\x93 The Treaty of Waitangi, granting British sovereignty in New Zealand, is signed.
    • \n
  • February 10 \xe2\x80\x93 Queen Victoria of the United Kingdom marries her cousin Prince Albert of Saxe-Coburg and Gotha.
    • \n
  • February 11 \xe2\x80\x93 Gaetano Donizetti\'s opera La fille du r\xc3\xa9giment premieres in Paris.
    • \n
  • March 1\n
      \n
    • William Hobson, the first Governor of New Zealand, suffers a stroke.
      • \n
    • Adolphe Thiers becomes prime minister of France.
      • \n
    \n
    • \n
  • March 9 \xe2\x80\x93 The Wilmington and Raleigh Railroad is completed from Wilmington, North Carolina to Weldon, North Carolina. At 161.5 miles (260 km), it is the world\'s longest railroad.[3]
    • \n

April\xe2\x80\x93June[edit]

\n
    \n
  • April \xe2\x80\x93 The Raleigh and Gaston Railroad is completed from Raleigh to near Weldon, North Carolina.[4]
    • \n
  • April 2 \xe2\x80\x93 The Washingtonian movement for teetotalism is founded by a group of alcoholics in Baltimore, Maryland.
    • \n
  • April 15 \xe2\x80\x93 King\'s College Hospital opens in London.
    • \n
  • May 1 \xe2\x80\x93 Britain issues the Penny Black, the world\'s first postage stamp; it becomes valid for the pre-payment of postage from May 6.
    • \n
  • May 7 \xe2\x80\x93 The Great Natchez Tornado: A massive tornado strikes Natchez, Mississippi during the early afternoon. Before it is over, 317 people are killed and 109 injured. It is the second deadliest tornado in U.S. history.
    • \n
  • May 21 \xe2\x80\x93 New Zealand is declared a British colony.
    • \n
  • June 7 \xe2\x80\x93 On the death of Frederick William III of Prussia he is succeeded on the throne of the Kingdom of Prussia (which he has ruled for more than 40 years) by his eldest son Frederick William IV.
    • \n
  • June 12\xe2\x80\x9323 \xe2\x80\x93 The World Anti-Slavery Convention is organised by the British and Foreign Anti-Slavery Society at Exeter Hall in London, England. Arguments over the exclusion of women from the convention have important ramifications for the movement for women\'s suffrage in the United States.
    • \n
\n

July\xe2\x80\x93September[edit]

\n
\n
\n
\n
\nJuly 4: RMS Britannia
\n
\n
\n
    \n
  • July 4 \xe2\x80\x93 The Cunard Line\'s 700-ton wooden paddlewheel steamer RMS Britannia departs from Liverpool, bound for Halifax, Nova Scotia on the first steam transatlantic passenger mail service.[5]
    • \n
  • July 15 \xe2\x80\x93 The Austrian Empire, the United Kingdom, the Kingdom of Prussia, and the Russian Empire sign the Convention of London with the Sublime Porte, ruler of the Ottoman Empire.
    • \n
  • July 21 \xe2\x80\x93 August Borsig\'s steam locomotive, the first built in Germany, competes against a Stephenson-built locomotive on the Berlin\xe2\x80\x93J\xc3\xbcterbog railroad; the Borsig locomotive wins by 10 minutes.
    • \n
  • July 23\n
      \n
    • Pedro II is declared "of age" prematurely and begins to reassert central control in Brazil.
      • \n
    • The Province of Canada is created by the Act of Union.
      • \n
    \n
    • \n
  • August 10 \xe2\x80\x93 Fortsas hoax: A number of book collectors gather in Binche, Belgium, to attend a non-existent book auction of the late "Count of Fortsas".
    • \n
  • September 10 \xe2\x80\x93 Ottoman and British troops bombard Beirut and land troops on the coast to pressure Egyptian Muhammad Ali to retreat from the country.
    • \n
  • September 16 \xe2\x80\x93 Joseph Strutt hands over the deeds and papers concerning the Derby Arboretum, which is to become England\'s first public park.
    • \n
  • September 30 \xe2\x80\x93 The frigate Belle-Poule arrives in Cherbourg, bringing back the remains of Napol\xc3\xa9on from Saint Helena to France. He is buried in Les Invalides.
    • \n

Date unknown[edit]

\n
    \n
  • Louis Agassiz publishes his Etudes sur les glaciers ("Study on Glaciers", 2 volumes), the first major scientific work to propose that the Earth has seen an ice age.
    • \n
  • The first English translation of Goethe\'s Theory of Colours by Charles Eastlake is published.
    • \n
  • J. M. W. Turner first displays his painting The Slave Ship.
    • \n
  • European traders set up the first tea gardens in the Indian subcontinent in Chittagong using Chinese tea plants from the Calcutta Botanical Garden.
    • \n
  • The first known photograph of Niagara Falls, a daguerreotype, is taken by English chemist Hugh Lee Pattinson.
    • \n
  • United States Census Bureau reports 6,000 free Negroes holding slaves in the nation.[citation needed]
    • \n
