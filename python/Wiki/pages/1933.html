1933 (MCMXXXIII) was a common year starting on Sunday (dominical letter A) of the Gregorian calendar, the 1933rd year of the Common Era (CE) and Anno Domini (AD) designations, the 933rd year of the 2nd millennium, the 33rd year of the 20th century, and the 4th year of the 1930s decade.

February[edit]

\n
Main article: February 1933
\n
    \n
  • February 1 \xe2\x80\x93 Adolf Hitler gives his "Proclamation to the German People" in Berlin.
    • \n
  • February 2 \xe2\x80\x93 A second international conference on disarmament ends without results. It tries to limit the army sizes of the major powers, while Germany is entitled to 200,000; Germany leaves the conference because a plan postpones the limitations for 4 years.
    • \n
  • February 5 \xe2\x80\x93 A mutiny starts on the Royal Netherlands Navy coastal defence ship De Zeven Provinci\xc3\xabn in the Dutch East Indies. After 6 days, it is bombed by a Dutch aircraft, killing 23, and the remaining mutineers surrender.
    • \n
  • February 6 \xe2\x80\x93 The Twentieth Amendment to the United States Constitution goes into effect.
    • \n
  • February 6\xe2\x80\x937 \xe2\x80\x93 Officers on the USS Ramapo record a 34-meter high sea-wave in the Pacific Ocean.
    • \n
  • February 9 \xe2\x80\x93 The King and Country debate: The Oxford Union student debating society in England passes a resolution stating, "That this House will in no circumstances fight for its King and country."[2]
    • \n
  • February 10 \xe2\x80\x93 The New York City-based Postal Telegraph Company introduces the first singing telegram.
    • \n
  • February 15 \xe2\x80\x93 In Miami, Giuseppe Zangara attempts to assassinate President-elect Franklin D. Roosevelt, but instead fatally wounds the Mayor of Chicago, Anton Cermak.
    • \n
  • February 17\n
      \n
    • Newsweek magazine is published for the first time in the United States.
      • \n
    • The Blaine Act passes the United States Senate, submitting the proposed Twenty-first Amendment to the Constitution to the states for ratification. The amendment is ratified on December 5, ending prohibition in the United States.
      • \n
    \n
    • \n
  • February 27 \xe2\x80\x93 Reichstag fire: Germany\'s parliament building in Berlin, the Reichstag building, is set on fire under controversial circumstances.
    • \n
  • February 28 \xe2\x80\x93 The Reichstag Fire Decree is passed in response to the Reichstag fire, nullifying many German civil liberties.
    • \n

March[edit]

\n
Main article: March 1933
\n
    \n
  • March 2 \xe2\x80\x93 The original film version of King Kong, starring Fay Wray, premieres at Radio City Music Hall and the RKO Roxy Theatre in New York City.
    • \n
  • March 3\n
      \n
    • Ching Yun University is established.
      • \n
    • Mount Rushmore National Memorial is dedicated.
      • \n
    • A powerful earthquake and tsunami hit Honsh\xc5\xab, Japan, killing approximately 3,000 people.
      • \n
    \n
    • \n
  • March 4\n
      \n
    • U.S. President Herbert Hoover is succeeded by Franklin D. Roosevelt (FDR), who in reference to the Great Depression, proclaims "The only thing we have to fear, is fear itself" in his inauguration speech. FDR is sworn in by Chief Justice Charles Evans Hughes. It is also the last time Inauguration Day in the United States occurs on March 4.
      • \n
    • Frances Perkins becomes United States Secretary of Labor, and the first female member of the United States Cabinet.
      • \n
    • The Parliament of Austria is suspended because of a quibble over procedure; Chancellor Engelbert Dollfuss initiates authoritarian rule by decree (see Austrofascism).
      • \n
    \n
    • \n
  • March 5\n
      \n
    • The Great Depression: President Franklin D. Roosevelt declares a "Bank holiday", closing all United States banks and freezing all financial transactions (the \'holiday\' ends on March 13).
      • \n
    • German election, 1933: National Socialists gain 43.9% of the votes.
      • \n
    \n
    • \n
  • March 6 \xe2\x80\x93 Mayor Anton Cermak of Chicago dies of the wound he received on February 15.
    • \n
  • March 9 \xe2\x80\x93 Great Depression: The United States Congress begins its first 100 days of enacting New Deal legislation.
    • \n
  • March 10 \xe2\x80\x93 The 6.4 Mw Long Beach earthquake shakes Southern California with a maximum Mercalli intensity of VIII (Severe), killing 115 people.
    • \n
  • March 12 \xe2\x80\x93 Great Depression: Franklin Delano Roosevelt addresses the nation for the first time as President of the United States, in the first of his "Fireside chats".
    • \n
  • March 15\n
      \n
    • The Dow Jones Industrial Average rises from 53.84 to 62.10. The day\'s gain of 15.34%, achieved during the depths of the Great Depression, remains to date as the largest 1-day percentage gain for the index.
      • \n
    • Austrian Chancellor Engelbert Dollfuss keeps members of the National Council from convening, starting the Austrofascist dictatorship.
      • \n
    \n
    • \n
  • March 20 \xe2\x80\x93 Dachau, the first Nazi concentration camp, is completed (it opens March 22).
    • \n
  • March 22 \xe2\x80\x93 President Franklin Roosevelt signs an amendment to the Volstead Act known as the Cullen\xe2\x80\x93Harrison Act, allowing the manufacture and sale of "3.2 beer" (3.2% alcohol by weight, approximately 4% alcohol by volume) and light wines.[3]
    • \n
  • March 23 \xe2\x80\x93 The Reichstag passes the Enabling Act, making Adolf Hitler dictator of Germany.
    • \n
  • March 24 \xe2\x80\x93 Jewish protesters in New York City call for a boycott of German goods in response to the persecution of German Jews by the Nazis.
    • \n
  • March 27 \xe2\x80\x93 Japan announces to leave the League of Nations (due to a cancelation period of exactly two years, the egression becomes effective March 27, 1935)[4]
    • \n
  • March 31 \xe2\x80\x93 The Civilian Conservation Corps is established with the mission of relieving rampant unemployment in the United States.
    • \n

  • April 4 \xe2\x80\x93 The American airship Akron crashes off the coast of New Jersey, killing 73 of its 76 crewmen. It is the worst aviation accident in history up to this date and until 1950.
    • \n
  • April 5\n
      \n
    • The International Court of Justice in The Hague decides that Greenland belongs to Denmark and condemns Norwegian landings on eastern Greenland. Norway submits to the decision.

      • \n

    • \n

      \n
    • Sale of some beer is legalized in the United States under the Cullen-Harrison Act of March 22, eight months before the full repeal of Prohibition in December.

      • \n
    \n
    • \n

    • \n
  • April 13 \xe2\x80\x93 The Children and Young Persons Act is passed in the United Kingdom.
    • \n
  • April 19 \xe2\x80\x93 The United States officially goes off the gold standard.

  • April 21 \xe2\x80\x93 Nazi Germany outlaws the kosher ritual shechita.
    • \n
  • April 24\n
      \n
    • Persecution of Jehovah\'s Witnesses in Nazi Germany begins with seizure of the Bible Students\' office in Magdeburg.
      • \n
    • Jewish physicians in Nazi Germany are excluded from official insurance schemes, forcing many to give up their practices.[6]
      • \n
    \n
    • \n
  • April 26\n
      \n
    • The Gestapo secret police are established in Nazi Germany by Hermann G\xc3\xb6ring.
      • \n
    • Editors of the Harvard Lampoon steal the Sacred Cod of Massachusetts from the State House (it is returned two days later).
      • \n
    \n
    • \n
  • April 27\n
      \n
    • The Jessop & Son department store in Nottingham, England is acquired by John Lewis Partnership (its first store outside of London).
      • \n
    • The Stahlhelm organization joins the Nazi party.
      • \n
