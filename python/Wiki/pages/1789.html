\n1789 (MDCCLXXXIX) was a common year starting on Thursday (dominical letter D) of the Gregorian calendar and a common year starting on Monday (dominical letter G) of the Julian calendar, the 1789th year of the Common Era (CE) and Anno Domini (AD) designations, the 789th year of the 2nd millennium, the 89th year of the 18th century, and the 10th and last year of the 1780s decade. Note that the Julian day for 1789 is 11 calendar days difference, which continued to be used from 1582 until the complete conversion of the Gregorian calendar was entirely done in 1929.

\n

Events[edit]

\n

January\xe2\x80\x93June[edit]

\n
    \n
  • January \xe2\x80\x93 Emmanuel Joseph Siey\xc3\xa8s publishes the pamphlet What Is the Third Estate? (Qu\'est-ce que le tiers-\xc3\xa9tat?), influential on the French Revolution.
    • \n
  • January 7 \xe2\x80\x93 United States presidential election, 1788\xe2\x80\x9389 and House of Representatives elections are held.
    • \n
  • January 21 \xe2\x80\x93 The first American novel, The Power of Sympathy or the Triumph of Nature Founded in Truth, is printed in Boston, Massachusetts. The anonymous author is William Hill Brown.
    • \n
  • January 23 \xe2\x80\x93 Georgetown University is founded in Washington, D.C., the first Roman Catholic college in the United States.
    • \n
\n
\n
\n
\n
\nFebruary 4: First President of the United States, George Washington, elected.
\n
\n
\n
    \n
  • February \xe2\x80\x93 King Gustav III of Sweden enforces the Union and Security Act, delivering the coup de grace to Sweden\'s 70-year-old parliamentarian system in favor of absolute monarchy.
    • \n
  • February 4 \xe2\x80\x93 George Washington is unanimously elected the first President of the United States by the United States Electoral College.
    • \n
  • March \xe2\x80\x93 First version of a graphic description of a slave ship (the Brookes) issued on behalf of the English Society for Effecting the Abolition of the Slave Trade.[1][2]
    • \n
  • March 4 \xe2\x80\x93 At Federal Hall in New York City, the 1st United States Congress meets and declares the new United States Constitution to be in effect. The bicameral United States Congress replaces the unicameral Congress of the Confederation as the legislature of the federal government of the United States.
    • \n
  • April 1 \xe2\x80\x93 At Federal Hall, the United States House of Representatives attains its first quorum and elects congressman Frederick Muhlenberg as the first Speaker of the House.
    • \n
  • April 6 \xe2\x80\x93 At Federal Hall, the United States Senate attains its first quorum and elects John Langdon of Pennsylvania as its first President pro tempore. Later that day, the Senate and the House of Representatives meet in joint session for the first time, and the electoral votes of the first U.S. Presidential election are counted. General George Washington is certified as President-elect and John Adams is certified as Vice-President elect.
    • \n
  • April 7 \xe2\x80\x93 Selim III (1789\xe2\x80\x931807) succeeds Abdul Hamid I (1773\xe2\x80\x931789) as Ottoman Sultan.
    • \n
  • April 21 \xe2\x80\x93 John Adams takes office as the first Vice President of the United States and begins presiding over the United States Senate.
    • \n
  • April 28 \xe2\x80\x93 Mutiny on the Bounty: Fletcher Christian leads the mutiny on the British Royal Navy ship HMS Bounty against Captain William Bligh in the Pacific Ocean.
    • \n
  • April 30 \xe2\x80\x93 George Washington is inaugurated at Federal Hall in New York City, beginning his term as the first President of the United States.
    • \n
  • May 5 \xe2\x80\x93 In France, the Estates-General convenes for the first time in 175 years.
    • \n
  • June - The Conjouring of Minas Gerais is the first attempt of Brazilian independence from Portugal.
    • \n
  • June 14 \xe2\x80\x93 Survivors of the mutiny on the Bounty, including Captain William Bligh and 18 others, reach Timor after a nearly 4,000-mile (6,400 km) journey in an open boat.
    • \n

\nApril 28: Mutiny on the Bounty.

\n
    \n
  • June 17 \xe2\x80\x93 In France, representatives of the Third Estate at the Estates-General declare themselves the National Assembly.
    • \n
  • June 20 \xe2\x80\x93 Tennis Court Oath is made in Versailles.
    • \n
  • June 23 \xe2\x80\x93 Louis XVI of France makes a conciliatory speech urging reforms to a joint session and orders the three estates to meet together.
    • \n

July\xe2\x80\x93December[edit]

\n
    \n
  • July \xe2\x80\x93 An estimated 150,000 of Paris\'s 600,000 people are without work.
    • \n
  • July 1 \xe2\x80\x93 The comic ballet La fille mal gard\xc3\xa9e choreographed by Jean Dauberval is first presented under the title Le ballet de la paille at the Grand Th\xc3\xa9\xc3\xa2tre de Bordeaux, in Bordeaux, France.
    • \n
  • July 9\n
      \n
    • At Versailles, the National Assembly reconstitutes itself as the National Constituent Assembly and begins preparations for what was to become the French Constitution of 1791.
      • \n
    • The Theater War officially ends in Scandinavia.
      • \n
    \n
    • \n
  • July 10 \xe2\x80\x93 Alexander Mackenzie reaches Mackenzie River Delta.
    • \n
  • July 11 \xe2\x80\x93 Louis XVI of France dismisses popular Chief Minister Jacques Necker.
    • \n
  • July 12 \xe2\x80\x93 An angry Parisian crowd, inflamed by a speech from journalist Camille Desmoulins, demonstrates against the King\xe2\x80\x99s decision to dismiss Minister Necker.
    • \n
  • July 13 \xe2\x80\x93 The people begin to seize arms for the defense of Paris.
    • \n
  • July 14 \xe2\x80\x93 The French Revolution (1789\xe2\x80\x931799) begins with the Storming of the Bastille: Citizens of Paris storm the fortress of the Bastille and free the only seven prisoners held. In rural areas, peasants attack manors of the nobility.
    • \n
\n
\n
\n
\n
\nJuly 14: Storming of the Bastille.
\n
\n
\n
    \n
  • July 27 \xe2\x80\x93 The first agency of the Federal government of the United States under the new Constitution, the Department of Foreign Affairs (from September 15 renamed the Department of State), is established.
    • \n
  • August 4 \xe2\x80\x93 In France, members of the Constituent Assembly take an oath to end feudalism and abandon their privileges.
    • \n
  • August 7 \xe2\x80\x93 The United States Department of War is established.[3]
    • \n
  • August 18 \xe2\x80\x93 The Li\xc3\xa8ge Revolution breaks out in the Prince-Bishopric of Li\xc3\xa8ge.
    • \n
  • August 21 \xe2\x80\x93 A proposal for a Bill of Rights is adopted by the United States House of Representatives.[4][5]
    • \n
  • August 26 \xe2\x80\x93 The Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen is proclaimed in France by the Constituent Assembly.
    • \n
  • August 28 \xe2\x80\x93 William Herschel discovers Enceladus, one of Saturn\'s moons.
    • \n
  • September 2 \xe2\x80\x93 The United States Department of the Treasury is founded.
    • \n
  • September 22 \xe2\x80\x93 Russo-Turkish War (1787\xe2\x80\x9392) \xe2\x80\x93 Battle of Rymnik: Alexander Suvorov roundly defeats 100,000 Turks.
    • \n
  • September 24 \xe2\x80\x93 The Judiciary Act of 1789 establishes the federal judiciary and the United States Marshals Service.[6]
    • \n
  • September 25 \xe2\x80\x93 The United States Congress proposes a set of 12 amendments for ratification by the states. Ratification for 10 of these proposals is completed on December 5, 1791, creating the United States Bill of Rights.
    • \n
  • September 29 \xe2\x80\x93 The U.S. Department of War establishes the nation\'s first regular army, with a strength of several hundred men.
    • \n
  • October 5 \xe2\x80\x93 Women\'s March on Versailles: Some 7,000 women march 12 miles (19 km) from Paris to the royal Palace of Versailles to demand action over high bread prices.
    • \n
  • October 10 \xe2\x80\x93 Physician Joseph-Ignace Guillotin proposes to the French National Assembly the adoption of more humane and egalitarian forms of capital punishment, including use of the guillotine.
    • \n
  • October 24 \xe2\x80\x93 Brabant revolutionaries cross the border from the Dutch Republic into the Austrian Netherlands as the first act of the Brabant Revolution; first public reading of the Manifesto of the People of Brabant declaring the independence of the Austrian Netherlands.
    • \n
  • October 27 \xe2\x80\x93 Austrian army beaten by Brabant revolutionaries at the Battle of Turnhout
    • \n
  • November 6 \xe2\x80\x93 Pope Pius VI appoints John Carroll the first Roman Catholic bishop in the United States.
    • \n
  • November 20 \xe2\x80\x93 New Jersey ratifies the United States Bill of Rights, the first state to do so.
    • \n
  • November 21 \xe2\x80\x93 North Carolina ratifies the United States Constitution and becomes the 12th U.S. state.
    • \n
  • November 26 \xe2\x80\x93 A national Thanksgiving Day is observed in the United States as recommended by President George Washington and approved by Congress.
    • \n
  • December 11 \xe2\x80\x93 The University of North Carolina, the oldest public university in the United States, is founded.
    • \n
  • December 23 \xe2\x80\x93 A leaflet circulated in France accuses marquis de Favras of plotting to rescue the royal family.
    • \n

Date unknown[edit]

\n
    \n
  • Joseph II, Holy Roman Emperor, decrees that all peasant labor obligations be converted into cash payments.
    • \n
  • Trait\xc3\xa9 \xc3\x89l\xc3\xa9mentaire de Chimie (Elementary Treatise of Chemistry), an influential chemistry textbook by Antoine Lavoisier, is published; translated into English in 1790, it comes to be considered the first modern chemical textbook.
    • \n
  • German chemist Martin Heinrich Klaproth discovers the element Uranium while studying the mineral pitchblende.
    • \n
  • The Bengal Presidency first establishes a penal colony in the Andaman Islands.
    • \n
  • Thomas Jefferson returns from Europe, bringing the first macaroni machine to the United States.
    • \n
  • Influenced by Dr. Benjamin Rush\'s argument against the excessive use of alcohol, about 200 farmers in a Connecticut community form a temperance movement in the United States.
    • \n
  • Fort Washington (Cincinnati, Ohio), is built to protect early U.S. settlements in the Northwest Territory.
    • \n
  • Former slave Olaudah Equiano\'s autobiography The Interesting Narrative of the Life of Olaudah Equiano, one of the earliest published works by a black writer, is published in London.[7]
    • \n
